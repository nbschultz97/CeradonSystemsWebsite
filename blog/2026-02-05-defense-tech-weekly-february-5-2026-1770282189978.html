Defense Tech Weekly: February 5, 2026

By Noah Schultz · Published February 4, 2026 · Ceradon Systems Weekly Insights

This week's defense news is dominated by advancements in autonomous systems, the growing integration of AI, and the persistent need for increased cybersecurity. From drone swarms and autonomous chemical weapon cleanup to the rise of AI-driven cyberattacks, the defense landscape is rapidly evolving. We also see continued investment in advanced technologies like photonics-electronics convergence and augmented reality, alongside ongoing efforts to streamline procurement and ramp up missile production.

Autonomous Systems Take Center Stage

The U.S. Army is actively pursuing autonomous solutions for hazardous tasks, exemplified by its search for the Autonomous Decontamination System (ADS). This system would utilize drones to "scrub vehicles, critical infrastructure and key terrain while allowing troops to protect themselves."[1] This initiative highlights the increasing demand for robotic systems that can mitigate risks in dangerous environments. The use of drones for chemical weapon cleanup signifies a strategic shift towards minimizing human exposure in CBRN (Chemical, Biological, Radiological, and Nuclear) scenarios.

Adding to the momentum around autonomous systems, Overland AI Inc. secured $100 million in funding to "help accelerate the use of robots and other autonomous systems across the US militaryâ€™s ground forces."[10] This substantial investment underscores the growing confidence in the potential of land-based robots for military applications.

Furthermore, Ondas is expanding its reach in the autonomous UAS (Unmanned Aircraft Systems) sector, securing an APAC defense contract[8] and launching a "unified lower-altitude to ground defense and security offering"[9] at the Singapore Airshow 2026. Ondas' approach allows for rapid responses to threats across various environments, signaling an increasing focus on integrated air and ground defense solutions.

The Rise of AI-Driven Threats and Defenses

The integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) is transforming both offensive and defensive capabilities in the cyber domain. Security leaders are warning about the "increasingly effective use of AI, and especially agentic AI, that will revolutionize the attack scenario."[3] Michael Freeman, head of threat intelligence at Armis, predicts that "By mid-2026, at least one major global enterprise will fall to a breach caused or significantly advanced by a fully autonomous agentic AI system."[3] This prediction serves as a stark reminder of the urgent need to bolster cybersecurity defenses against AI-powered threats.

However, the use of AI in defense is not without its challenges. The Pentagon is reportedly in disagreement with Anthropic over "safeguards that would prevent the government from deploying its technology to target weapons autonomously and conduct U.S. domestic surveillance."[7] This clash underscores the ethical and practical considerations surrounding the deployment of AI in sensitive military applications, particularly concerning autonomous weapons systems and domestic surveillance.

Investment in Advanced Technologies and Drone Dominance

The defense industry continues to invest heavily in advanced technologies to enhance situational awareness, decision-making, and combat effectiveness. The photonics-electronics convergence technology market is projected to reach USD 104.26 billion by 2032, driven by the limitations of conventional electronics in handling increasing data loads, AI processing, and advanced defense systems.[4]

Vuzix highlighted at CES 2026 a collaboration with Collins Aerospace on a "fully functional military helmet"[6] integrating augmented reality (AR) capabilities. This development aims to shrink battlefield decision times by providing soldiers with enhanced situational awareness and real-time information. The market shift towards AR-enabled solutions is estimated at $161 billion, demonstrating the significant potential of this technology in modern warfare.

The Pentagon is also heavily invested in achieving "Drone Dominance," selecting 25 companies to participate in a $1 billion initiative focused on developing small, cheap attack drones.[14] The goal is to field swarms of low-cost, one-way attack drones, each costing just a few thousand dollars.[5] This initiative reflects a growing emphasis on distributed, autonomous, and cost-effective solutions for modern warfare. The global drone payload market is projected to reach US$ 39.7 Billion by 2033, driven by the demand for advanced, lightweight sensors, AI integration, and ISR capabilities.[2]

Other Key Developments

Beyond these major trends, several other developments are noteworthy:

  • Congress has approved an $839 billion defense budget for fiscal year 2026.[11]
  • BAE Systems secured a $195 million contract to deliver the ACV-30, a 30mm cannon-equipped variant of the Amphibious Combat Vehicle, to the Marine Corps.[15]
  • Raytheon is ramping up missile production following Pentagon deals.[13]

Key Takeaways

  • **Autonomy is paramount:** The demand for autonomous systems, from decontamination drones to ground robots, is increasing across various military applications.
  • **AI presents both opportunities and threats:** While AI offers significant advantages in defense, it also poses new cybersecurity risks and raises ethical concerns regarding autonomous weapons.
  • **Advanced technologies are crucial:** Investments in photonics-electronics convergence, augmented reality, and drone technology are driving innovation and transforming the modern battlefield.
  • **Drone dominance is a priority:** The Pentagon is actively pursuing the development of low-cost, high-impact drone swarms to enhance its offensive capabilities.
