Defense Tech Weekly: February 5, 2026

Published February 4, 2026 · Ceradon Systems Weekly Insights

This week's defense news highlights a surge in autonomous systems, AI integration, and a renewed focus on missile production, painting a picture of a rapidly evolving defense landscape. From autonomous drones designed for chemical weapon cleanup to AI-powered cyberattacks, the industry is grappling with both the opportunities and challenges presented by emerging technologies. Let's delve into the key trends shaping the future of defense.

The Rise of Autonomous Systems: Drones Take Center Stage

Autonomous systems, particularly drones, are becoming increasingly prominent in defense strategies. The US Army is actively seeking "Autonomous Decontamination System, or ADS," drones to clean up chemical weapons, aiming to scrub vehicles, critical infrastructure, and key terrain while safeguarding troops. This initiative, detailed by Defense News, underscores the growing reliance on unmanned systems for hazardous tasks. The ADS would allow troops to protect themselves while robots handle the hazardous work of decontamination.

Furthermore, the Pentagon is investing heavily in small, cheap attack drones, selecting 25 firms for a competition focused on fielding swarms of low-cost, one-way attack drones costing just a few thousand dollars apiece. This "Drone Dominance Initiative," as highlighted by the Washington Times, signifies a shift towards affordable, expendable unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) for offensive operations. This initiative aligns with the broader trend of increasing drone payload market, which Astute Analytica projects will reach US$ 39.7 Billion by 2033, driven by defense, AI integration, and autonomous systems.

Ondas Holdings also secured a strategic defense contract in the Asia-Pacific region for autonomous UAS. According to Stock Titan, this contract is part of Ondas' global expansion and highlights the increasing demand for advanced unmanned systems in the defense sector. Ondas' American Robotics Optimus Drone has also been approved for rapid federal procurement via the DCMA Blue UAS Cleared List, further solidifying its position in the autonomous systems market.

AI's Double-Edged Sword: Enhancing Capabilities, Amplifying Threats

Artificial intelligence (AI) continues to permeate all aspects of defense, offering both unprecedented capabilities and novel threats. DefenseScoop reports that the Pentagon is tracking more than a million unique users on its new commercial generative AI platform, GenAI.mil, demonstrating widespread adoption across military branches. This platform provides access to advanced AI tools for various applications, from intelligence analysis to operational planning.

However, the integration of AI is not without its challenges. SecurityWeek warns of the increasing threat of AI-powered cyberattacks. Michael Freeman, head of threat intelligence at Armis, predicts that "By mid-2026, at least one major global enterprise will fall to a breach caused or significantly advanced by a fully autonomous agentic AI system." This highlights the urgent need for robust cybersecurity measures to defend against sophisticated AI-driven threats.

Furthermore, Reuters reports that the Pentagon is clashing with AI developer Anthropic over safeguards that would prevent the government from deploying its technology to target weapons autonomously and conduct U.S. domestic surveillance. This highlights the ethical and legal complexities surrounding the use of AI in defense, particularly regarding autonomous weapons systems and privacy concerns. The need for clear guidelines and regulations governing the development and deployment of AI in military applications is paramount.

Investment and Production: Boosting Missile Capabilities and Modernizing Forces

Despite potential clashes over AI ethics, investment in defense continues to grow. Congress has passed the fiscal 2026 defense spending bill, approving $839 billion in funding for the Pentagon, according to Air & Space Forces Magazine. This substantial budget reflects the ongoing commitment to modernizing military capabilities and addressing emerging threats.

Raytheon is set to ramp up missile production in response to Pentagon deals, as reported by Military Times. This increase in production capacity is crucial for maintaining a strong deterrent posture and replenishing stockpiles. The announcement comes weeks after former President Trump threatened to nix the company's government contracts, highlighting the ongoing political dynamics influencing defense spending and procurement.

BAE Systems also secured a $195 million Marine Corps ACV contract to deliver the ACV-30, a 30mm cannon-equipped variant of the Amphibious Combat Vehicle, according to ExecutiveBiz. This contract underscores the continued investment in modernizing amphibious warfare capabilities and equipping the Marine Corps with advanced combat vehicles.

Photonics-Electronics Convergence: Overcoming Limitations

The limitations of conventional electronics in data, AI, and defense systems are driving the growth of the photonics-electronics convergence technology market, which is projected to cross USD 104.26 billion by 2032, according to PR Newswire. This convergence promises to enhance the speed, efficiency, and performance of defense systems, enabling more advanced capabilities in areas such as sensing, communication, and computing.

Augmented Reality for Battlefield Decision Making

Vuzix highlighted at CES 2026 a fully functional military helmet in collaboration with Collins Aerospace, showcasing the potential of augmented reality (AR) to shrink battlefield decision times, according to GlobeNewswire. The company is focusing on waveguide performance, system-level integration, and manufacturability to support defense customers evaluating AR-enabled solutions across land, air, and mission environments. This collaboration demonstrates the growing interest in AR technology to provide soldiers with real-time information and enhanced situational awareness.

Autonomous Driving for SDF Facilities

TIER IV has been selected for an Acquisition, Technology and Logistics Agency (ATLA) study exploring autonomous driving applications for Japan Self-Defense Forces (SDF) facilities, according to PR Newswire. This study aims to assess the feasibility and benefits of deploying autonomous vehicles for logistics, transportation, and security within SDF facilities. The selection of TIER IV, a pioneering force behind open-source software for autonomous driving, highlights the growing interest in leveraging commercial autonomous technologies for military applications.

Key Takeaways

  • Autonomous systems, particularly drones, are playing an increasingly significant role in defense, from chemical weapon cleanup to offensive operations.
  • AI is transforming defense capabilities, but also introduces new cybersecurity threats and ethical concerns.
  • Increased defense spending is driving modernization efforts, with a focus on missile production and advanced combat vehicles.
  • Photonics-electronics convergence is emerging as a key technology for overcoming the limitations of conventional electronics in defense systems.
  • Augmented reality and autonomous driving technologies are being explored for their potential to enhance battlefield decision-making and improve logistics within defense facilities.
